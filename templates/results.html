Would you be a fully informed conservative or progressive?

Where would your fully informed self fall on the spectrum from conservative to progressive, compared to some prominent political figures?

Think of it this way: for each of the questions we've seen, one of the answers (yes/no) is the one a progressive would likely give (e.g., yes, diversity is good; no, don't repeal ACA, etc.); the other one, is the answer a conservative (of the kind currently dominating the Republican party) would likely give.

So, if we add up the probabilities that you would give the 'progressive' answer to each of these questions, we get a number between 0 and 10, with 10 representing a 100% probability of giving the progressive answer to each.

Based on this, your 'progressivism score' would be {{ liberal_score }} out of 10, making your informed self a {{ liberal_position }}.

Consider a simple example:

Say I want to predict how much you earn but all I know about you is your age. I might ask lots of people their age and their income, and then note the relationship.

Maybe it turns out that, in general, the older you are, the more you make (until retirement age, say). If I describe that relationship using an equation — being of this age means likely earning this much — I now have a model that I can use to predict your income.

Now, perhaps a better model would also look at people’s level of education, years of relevant work experience, and so forth. And when we include further variables like this, we can do something else: we can estimate how much you would have earned, had you been different along one of the variables involved.

For example, maybe you never went to university. How much would you have earned now if you did? We plug in all of the relevant values for you into the model (your age and so on), but set the level of the education variable to ‘university educated’ and, voilà, the model estimates how much someone who’s just like you, except for being more highly educated, would likely earn.

In our model of informed political opinion, we do something similar. We ask you to plug in a variety of demographic and socioeconomic variables. But the model has an additional variable that you don’t get to set: how politically informed you are. We’ve already set that one to the maximum value. That's how we estimate what political attitudes you likely would have held, had you been fully informed.

The reason we estimate your informed vote on the basis of demographic variables while excluding attitudinal and values variables is not that the latter aren’t predictive of how you will vote (they are); it’s that they are not predictive of how you would vote, if fully informed.

This is because, if you were more informed, it's quite possible that you would embrace different values and have different political attitudes from what you currently do.

That means that, if you include attitudinal and values variables in the model, you hold fixed things that in fact vary with increased knowledge, which in turn would bias your estimate. In the literature, this is known as 'controlling for a mediator'. Julia Rohrer has a good and accessible paper on this, for those interested in more details.

You do it by asking large numbers of people to, for example, pair up named individuals with their political offices, or about political platforms, political practices, and so forth.

This is something political scientists have been doing for some time, and it turns out that, by asking people a few, well-chosen factual questions, you can derive a good measure of how politically informed they are.

In light of this tradition, the main surveys of political attitudes, such as the American National Election Studies and the British Election Study, now generally include a number of such factual questions in their national surveys.

Researchers can then use these to define what you can think of as a 'political knowledge score'. That’s the score we set to the maximum in our model, when estimating your fully informed opinions.

Those interested in more details may consult the central books on ‘information effects’ by Michael X. Delli Carpini and Scott Keeter and Scott Althaus. Studies on information effects in electoral contexts specifically have been conducted in the US, Canada, Sweden, Denmark, and for the European Parliament, to name a few examples.
